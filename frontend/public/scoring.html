StARCStock Thesis Arc

How the Score Works

The STARC score is fully deterministic. No language model decides your number. Every input, every weight, and every math step is defined in Python you can read — and this page is the plain-English version.

1The one-line version

Every stock starts at 50. Confirmed signals push the score up; flagged signals push it down. The final number is clamped to [0, 100]. That's the whole architecture — the rest of this page is just what counts as a signal and how much each one moves the needle.

Why 50 is the base: a score of exactly 50 means no evidence either way — a brand-new thesis on a quiet stock. It's the neutral starting line, not a passing grade.

2Signals — where the evidence comes from

When you click Evaluate, STARC pulls fresh data across nine signal families:

  • Price signals — day, week, and month % change; volume vs. 10-day average; 20-day vs. 50-day moving average; distance from 52-week high/low.
  • Valuation — P/E, forward P/E, PEG, P/S, P/B, EV/EBITDA, analyst target price.
  • Financial health — debt-to-equity, current ratio, ROE, margins, free cash flow, revenue growth.
  • Fundamentals — EPS actual vs. estimate, surprise %, revenue growth.
  • Ownership — institutional %, short interest %, analyst count, analyst recommendation.
  • Insider activity — Form 4 filings over the last 90 days.
  • Filings — 8-K filing count over the last 90 days.
  • News — recent headlines mapped to thesis categories.
  • Trend labels — pre-computed accelerating / stable / decelerating tags on revenue, margin, and debt. These stabilize the score against single-quarter noise.

Each signal gets routed to one or more of your thesis points by deterministic rules, then labeled confirmed (supports the point) or flagged (contradicts it) with a confidence score.

3The multipliers — how much each point moves the score

A thesis point's contribution to the score is:

base × category_weight × importance × conviction × confidence

Category weight

Not all thesis categories matter equally to your strategy. A growth investor weights Growth Trajectory heaviest; a value investor weights Valuation and Financial Health heaviest. STARC picks regime-aware defaults from your investor profile; you can override them in Settings.

Importance multiplier

ImportanceMultiplierWhen to use
Standard1.0×Default for most points.
Important1.5×Central to your reasoning; pay attention if it breaks.
Critical2.0×Load-bearing. If it breaks, the whole thesis may be wrong.

Conviction multiplier

ConvictionMultiplierSignal
Neutral1.0×You have no strong view.
Liked1.2×You endorse this point as part of your case.
Disliked1.2×You disagree; strengthens bearish signals.
Frozen (locked)2.0×Core conviction. Breaking a frozen point triggers a red-banner alert.

Confidence

The signal-routing layer attaches a confidence value (0–1) to each mapping based on how strongly the evidence supports the category. A clear month-over-month price decline scores higher confidence than a mild, ambiguous one.

4Regime + sector adjustments

Two quiet adjustments prevent one-size-fits-all scoring:

  • Investor regime — Growth, Value, Income, Defensive. Each profile shifts category weights. A Value investor's Valuation weight is higher than a Growth investor's.
  • Sector presets — SaaS defaults differ from consumer staples differ from banks. A bank thesis weights Financial Health more than a SaaS thesis does, because balance-sheet strength matters more for banks.

Both kick in automatically on stock creation. You see the effective weights in Settings and can override them per-portfolio.

5Signal decay

Old signals weigh less than recent ones. A news headline from 60 days ago counts roughly half of one from this week. This prevents stale signals from anchoring the score while still honoring the longer-term picture.

6Worked example

Suppose you hold MSFT with 5 selected thesis points. You run Evaluate. Here is what happens:

  1. Start at base 50.
  2. Growth point (Standard, Neutral) — Azure revenue grew 30% YoY. Confirmed, confidence 0.8. Contribution: +10 × 1.0 × 1.0 × 1.0 × 0.8 = +8.
  3. Moat point (Critical, Frozen) — M365 enterprise retention held above 95%. Confirmed, confidence 0.9. Contribution: +10 × 1.0 × 2.0 × 2.0 × 0.9 = +36.
  4. Valuation point (Important, Liked) — P/E of 34 trades above peer median. Flagged, confidence 0.7. Contribution: −10 × 1.0 × 1.5 × 1.2 × 0.7 = −12.6.
  5. Risk point (Standard, Neutral) — antitrust lawsuit filed. Flagged, confidence 0.6. Contribution: −10 × 1.0 × 1.0 × 1.0 × 0.6 = −6.
  6. Cash-flow point (Standard, Liked) — FCF margin expanded. Confirmed, confidence 0.5. Contribution: +10 × 1.0 × 1.0 × 1.2 × 0.5 = +6.
Final: 50 + 8 + 36 − 12.6 − 6 + 6 = 81.4 → rounded to 81.

The Frozen + Critical moat point dominates the arithmetic. That is by design — breaking a locked critical point is the loudest signal STARC can fire.

7Zones

The final score maps to one of four zones on the gauge:

0–40 Breaking
40–60 Pressure
60–75 Holding
75–100 Intact
RedYellowLimeGreen

Zones are thresholds for your own attention budget, not trading signals. A score of 42 doesn't mean "sell"; it means "look carefully and decide." A score of 78 doesn't mean "buy more"; it means "the reasons still hold today."

8What the score is not

  • Not a price forecast. Our 2020–2024 backtest across 99 S&P 500 stocks found no reliable relationship between score and forward returns. The score measures thesis coherence, not alpha.
  • Not a buy or sell signal. STARC tells you whether your reasons still hold. The decision to act is yours.
  • Not an AI judgement. The engine is Python. LLMs are used upstream to draft thesis points and interpret news, but the scoring math is deterministic and auditable.
  • Not a consensus score. It's scored against your selected points. Two investors holding the same stock will get different STARC scores if their theses differ.
Use the score as a conversation starter with yourself. When it drops, ask: is my thesis wrong, or did the market just move? When it rises, ask: is this genuine confirmation, or am I anchoring?
