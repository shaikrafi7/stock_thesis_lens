The Product

A thesis is the set of reasons why you own a stock. Instead of "I like Apple," a thesis is "I own Apple because (1) services revenue is growing 15% annually, (2) installed base is 2B+ and sticky, (3) gross margins are expanding despite manufacturing headwinds." A clear thesis lets you know what specific events or data would make you sell—when your assumptions break, you exit. Without a thesis, you're making emotional guesses. With one, you're making systematic decisions.
A spreadsheet is static. Notion is flexible but unstructured. ThesisArc is built specifically for thesis investing: it scores your theses against internal consistency, ties market events to your specific assumptions, tracks how your thesis evolves over time, and benchmarks your holdings against your stated reasoning. You get structure + AI assist + portfolio-linked evaluation in one tool. No more jumping between doc, portfolio, and news tabs.
AI generates a draft thesis to accelerate framework-building, not to replace your thinking. You then edit, delete, lock, and customize every point until it matches your actual reasoning. Most users delete or heavily rewrite 30–40% of AI-generated points. The AI is a starting scaffold; you build the actual thesis. It's like having a research assistant who writes a first draft—you still have to think hard about the final version.
The score measures thesis coherence: how well your thesis points support each other, how clearly you've articulated assumptions, how thoroughly you've addressed counterarguments. A 92-score thesis is internally consistent and well-reasoned. It's not a prediction of stock price. It doesn't tell you whether the stock will go up. It tells you whether your reasoning is solid and whether you've thought through the risks. We're working on backtesting to show how past theses would have performed, but that's separate from the coherence score.
Review your theses quarterly or when major events hit. Quarterly aligns with earnings season and macro shifts. More frequent reviews can lead to overtrading; less frequent means you miss major assumption breaks. If you read news that directly challenges one of your thesis points—earnings miss, regulatory change, competitive threat—that's time to evaluate. The goal is systematic discipline, not daily anxiety.

Trust & Accuracy

The evaluation is accurate at measuring thesis coherence—does the thesis hold together logically? But accuracy at predicting stock price is a different question we're still answering with backtesting. We're honest about the limitations: the AI can miss context, may score weak reasoning as confident if it's well-articulated, and reflects biases in training data. Always use the score as a starting point for your own critical review, not as gospel truth.
Don't use the score as a trading signal. Use it as a diagnostic: "Are my reasons clear and well-tested?" If your score drops significantly after evaluation, that's a signal to re-examine your thesis and market changes—it's not a sell signal. Buy/sell decisions should come from your actual thesis breaking (your assumptions changing), not from the score alone. The score is one input, not the decision.

Practical

A good thesis point is: (1) Specific—not "Apple is innovative" but "Apple services revenue growing 15%+ annually for 3 years"; (2) Falsifiable—you could learn data that proves it wrong; (3) Material—it would change your buy/hold/sell decision if it breaks; (4) Supportable—you can cite evidence. Avoid vague statements, unprovable hunches, and points that are nice-to-haves rather than core reasons for owning the stock.
3–7 points is typical. Fewer than 3 suggests you haven't thought through why you own it; more than 10 suggests you're over-building the case and diluting focus. Focus on the points that would actually change your mind. If point #8 doesn't matter for your sell decision, cut it. Quality over quantity. The goal is a thesis you can defend and track, not a comprehensive investment report.
