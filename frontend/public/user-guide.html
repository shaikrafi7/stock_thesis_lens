What ThesisArc Is — and Isn't

ThesisArc is a thesis accountability tool. It helps you write down why you own each stock, then tracks whether the reasons still hold as evidence arrives. It is not a stock picker, not a robo-advisor, and not a system for generating excess returns.

What it helps with
  • Forcing clarity on your investment reasons
  • Spotting when a thesis starts to contradict the evidence
  • Journaling decision changes so you can learn from them
  • Reducing emotional, unstructured holding
What it does not do
  • Predict stock prices
  • Generate alpha — our 2020-2024 backtest showed no unconditional edge
  • Tell you what to buy or sell
  • Replace an advisor or your own judgment

The conviction score measures thesis coherence — how well your reasons hold together against evidence. A high score means your thesis is internally consistent; it does not mean the stock will go up. Backtesting over 99 S&P 500 stocks across 2020-2024 found no reliable relationship between score and forward returns. See Scoring System for detail.

1. Getting Started

Creating an Account

Visit ThesisArc and click Sign Up. You'll be guided to enter your email address, create a username, and set a secure password (minimum 6 characters recommended). After registration, you'll be automatically logged in and taken to the onboarding flow.

Logging In

Use the Sign In tab to enter your email and password. Your session persists across browser refreshes and will remain active until you explicitly log out or your authentication token expires (typically after 30 days of inactivity).

Onboarding & Profile Setup

On your first login, you'll see the Investor Profile Wizard — a guided questionnaire that captures your investing style, risk tolerance, and behavioral preferences. This is crucial: your profile determines which behavioral biases ThesisArc will help you recognize and manage.

Why this matters: Understanding your archetype helps you make better decisions. The wizard takes 2-3 minutes and shapes your entire experience.

Your First Look at the Dashboard

After profile setup, you'll land on your Dashboard — the command center showing portfolio health, holdings, and key metrics. On first use it will be empty. The next step is adding your first stock.

3. Managing Portfolios

ThesisArc supports unlimited portfolios so you can organize your investments by strategy, risk level, or any other dimension that matters to you.

Portfolio Isolation

Each portfolio is completely isolated. A stock in one portfolio doesn't appear in another. Same with thesis data, evaluations, and morning briefings. You can have:

  • One portfolio for your retirement core
  • Another for speculative swing trades
  • A third for dividend income
  • Etc.

Each gets its own thesis health score and briefing.

Default Portfolio

Every account comes with a Default portfolio. You can rename it or add stocks to it, but you cannot delete it. It always exists as a fallback.

Creating a New Portfolio

  1. Look at the top-left header where it shows your current portfolio name (e.g., "Default")
  2. Click on the portfolio name to open the portfolio dropdown menu
  3. At the bottom of the dropdown, click + New Portfolio
  4. Type a descriptive name (e.g., "Tech Plays", "Dividend Income", "Long-term Core")
  5. Click Add
  6. You'll automatically switch to the new portfolio; its dashboard will load immediately

Switching Portfolios

Click the portfolio name in the header and select any portfolio from the dropdown. The entire dashboard, briefing, and stock list update instantly to reflect your selection.

Renaming a Portfolio

In the portfolio dropdown, hover over any portfolio name. A pencil icon appears. Click it to rename the portfolio inline. Press Enter to save.

Deleting a Portfolio

In the portfolio dropdown, hover over any non-default portfolio and a trash icon appears. Click the trash to permanently delete the portfolio, all its stocks, all theses, and all evaluations.

Warning: Deletion is permanent and cannot be undone. The Default portfolio cannot be deleted.

Portfolio Permissions

Currently, all portfolios are private to your account. Portfolio sharing is planned for future releases.

4. Adding Stocks

Add a Single Stock

In the Portfolio Stocks section on the dashboard, type a stock ticker symbol (e.g., AAPL, MSFT, NVDA) in the input field. Then either:

  • Press Enter to add immediately, or
  • Click the + Add button

ThesisArc will:

  1. Look up the company information (name, sector, industry, market cap, logo)
  2. AI-generate an initial thesis with approximately 18 thesis points spread across 6 categories
  3. Automatically run an evaluation and score your initial thesis (0-100)
  4. Redirect you to the stock detail page

Add Multiple Stocks at Once

Type comma-separated tickers: AAPL, NVDA, MSFT, GOOG. Each stock is added sequentially, and you'll be taken to the first stock's detail page.

Invalid or Delisted Tickers

If you enter a ticker that doesn't exist or has been delisted, ThesisArc shows an error message with suggestions for similar ticker symbols. Verify and re-enter.

Removing a Stock

In the stocks table on the dashboard, hover over a stock's row and a trash icon appears on the far right. Click to remove the stock from your portfolio. This also deletes all its thesis data and evaluations.

Note: You can re-add a stock later, but all thesis history will be lost.

6. Thesis Manager

The thesis is the core of ThesisArc. It's a structured set of falsifiable statements explaining exactly why you own a stock.

Six Thesis Categories

Every thesis point belongs to one of these categories:

CategoryExamples
Competitive MoatBrand strength, network effects, switching costs, patents, proprietary technology
Growth TrajectoryRevenue growth %, market expansion, new product launches, geographic rollout
ValuationP/E vs. peers, price-to-sales, discount to intrinsic value, analyst targets
Financial HealthCash flow, debt levels, profitability trends, balance sheet strength
Ownership & ConvictionInsider buying, institutional ownership, analyst consensus upgrades
Risks & Bear CaseCompetition threats, regulatory risks, execution risks, macroeconomic sensitivity

AI-Generated Theses

When you add a stock, ThesisArc automatically generates ~18 thesis points using AI analysis of news, earnings reports, and market data. Click Regenerate Thesis anytime to get fresh AI-generated points.

Smart regeneration: When you regenerate, your manually-added points are preserved. Only AI-generated unfrozen points are replaced. Frozen core convictions are always kept.

Thesis Point Structure

Each point displays:

  • Checkbox — toggle whether this point is included in evaluation (minimum 3 required)
  • Category tag — color-coded label (e.g., "Competitive Moat")
  • Point text — the actual statement (should be falsifiable)
  • Importance indicator — star (standard) or lightning bolt (high importance)
  • Conviction indicators — thumbs up (like), thumbs down (dislike), lock icon (frozen)
  • Actions — pencil (edit), X (delete)

Selecting Points for Evaluation

Only selected (checked) thesis points are evaluated. You must select at least 3 points. Use the Select All / Deselect All buttons at the top to toggle all at once.

Strategy: Deselect points you're uncertain about before evaluation. You're not deleting them — just saying "don't score this one yet."

Adding Manual Thesis Points

Click the + button on any category header. A text input appears. Type your point (minimum 10 characters) and press Enter or click Add.

Manual points are never auto-deleted on thesis regeneration. They're your personal insights.

Editing a Thesis Point

Hover over any point and click the pencil icon. Edit the text inline and press Enter to save. If the point is frozen, you'll see a lock indicator.

Deleting a Thesis Point

Hover and click the X icon. If the point is frozen (a core conviction), you'll need to unfreeze it first. A confirmation dialog appears before deletion.

Importance Levels

Click the star icon on any point to toggle importance:

StateVisualMeaning
StandardNo icon / dimmed starNormal weight in scoring
ImportantFilled star iconBoosts weight by 1.5x in evaluation
CriticalLightning bolt iconHighest weight — treated as core

Conviction Signals

Show ThesisArc your real conviction by using the conviction buttons:

Like (Thumbs Up)

You believe this point is particularly strong. Click the thumbs-up icon. The point's signal is boosted by 1.2x in evaluation. Click again to toggle back to neutral.

Dislike (Thumbs Down)

You're skeptical about this point — you included it but you doubt it. Click the thumbs-down icon. The opposing signal (bear case) is boosted 1.2x. This flags your doubt without deleting the point. Click again to toggle back to neutral.

Lock / Freeze (Lock Icon)

Mark this as a core conviction — a reason you'd never sell, no matter what. Click the lock icon to freeze a point.

  • Frozen points cannot be deselected before evaluation
  • Frozen points are preserved on thesis regeneration
  • If a frozen point is broken during evaluation, it counts as 2x penalty instead of normal
  • A broken frozen point triggers a red banner alert: "Core Conviction Under Pressure"
Use freezing strategically. Only freeze 2-3 truly core convictions. Over-freezing dilutes the signal.

Generating & Running an Evaluation

Click the Evaluate (X selected) button at the bottom. ThesisArc:

  1. Analyzes each selected point against current market data, news, and fundamentals
  2. Assigns each point a status: Confirmed, Flagged, or Neutral
  3. Calculates a weighted score (0-100)
  4. Displays results with explanations

9. Scoring System

ThesisArc's scoring system is transparent, repeatable, and rooted in fundamental logic. Understanding the score mechanics helps you write stronger theses.

Base Score Calculation

Each evaluation run produces a final score (0-100) through a multi-step process:

Step 1: Point-Level Evaluation

For each selected thesis point, AI assigns one of three verdicts:

VerdictSignalPoints
ConfirmedMarket data supports this point+10 base
FlaggedMarket data contradicts this point-10 base
NeutralNo strong signal either way0 pts

Step 2: Apply Importance Multiplier

The importance level (standard, important, critical) scales the base points:

ImportanceMultiplierEffect
Standard1.0xNo change to base points
Important1.5xBoost by 50%
Critical2.0xDouble the points

Step 3: Apply Conviction Multiplier

Your conviction signals (like/dislike) further scale the outcome:

SignalMultiplierEffect
Neutral (no button)1.0xNo change
Like (thumbs up)1.2xBoost bullish signal by 20%
Dislike (thumbs down)1.2xBoost bearish signal by 20%
Frozen (lock)2.0xDouble all points (confirmed or flagged)

Step 4: Sum and Normalize

Total all weighted points (confirmed minus flagged) and map to 0-100 scale. The final score lands in one of four zones:

  • 0-40 (Red): Breaking — thesis contradicted by evidence
  • 40-60 (Yellow): Under Pressure — deteriorating confidence
  • 60-75 (Lime): Holding — thesis mostly intact
  • 75-100 (Green): Thesis Intact — thesis coherent with evidence

Example Walkthrough

Say you have 5 selected thesis points:

  1. Point A (Growth): Confirmed, Standard, Neutral → +10
  2. Point B (Valuation): Flagged, Important, Dislike → -10 × 1.5 × 1.2 = -18
  3. Point C (Moat): Confirmed, Critical, Like → +10 × 2.0 × 1.2 = +24
  4. Point D (Risk): Neutral, Standard, Neutral → 0
  5. Point E (Cash Flow): Confirmed, Important, Neutral → +10 × 1.5 = +15

Total: +10 - 18 + 24 + 0 + 15 = +31 raw points. Normalized to 0-100 scale, this might yield a final score of 62/100 (Under Pressure).

Frozen Point Alerts

If any frozen point (core conviction) is flagged, a red banner appears: "Core Conviction Under Pressure: [Point name] is broken." This is the strongest sell signal ThesisArc produces.

Score History

Each evaluation is timestamped and stored. The Score History chart on the stock detail page shows the trend over time. You can see whether your thesis is strengthening, weakening, or stable across multiple evaluations.

11. Tips & Best Practices

Build a Strong Thesis

  • Be specific and falsifiable. Instead of "Company is growing," write "Revenue will grow >20% YoY based on new product adoption." A thesis point should be something you could prove or disprove with data.
  • Cover all six categories. A thesis with only growth points and no risk assessment is blind. Include Competitive Moat, Growth, Valuation, Financial Health, Ownership, and Risks.
  • Use AI as a starting point, not the final word. The auto-generated thesis gets you to 18 points fast. Add manual points based on your research and conviction. Your insights should dominate.
  • Be honest about your uncertainty. Use the "Dislike" signal on points you included but doubt. This flags your ambivalence without deleting the point.

Use Freezing Strategically

  • Freeze only 2-3 true core convictions. These are reasons you'd never sell the stock, no matter what short-term volatility happens. If you freeze everything, the signal becomes noise.
  • A frozen break is the ultimate sell signal. When a core conviction fails evaluation, pay attention. This is ThesisArc telling you your fundamental thesis is broken.
  • Review frozen points quarterly. As your thesis evolves, some frozen points may no longer be core. Unfreeze them if your conviction changes.

Maintain Discipline

  • Check the Morning Briefing daily. Takes 30 seconds. Keeps you informed of relevant news before market open.
  • Re-evaluate at least weekly. Thesis scores drift as market conditions change. Weekly evaluations keep your scores current.
  • Act on red flags. If a stock stays in the Breaking zone across two consecutive evaluations, take it seriously. Either update your thesis with new supporting points, or question whether the thesis still holds at all.
  • Separate conviction from comfort. A thesis can be strong even if the stock is down. Don't conflate price movement with thesis validity.

Organize with Portfolios

  • Separate core holdings from speculation. Create a "Core Long-term" portfolio for your retirement stocks and a "Trading Ideas" portfolio for speculation. Each portfolio gets its own score and briefing.
  • Use descriptive portfolio names. "Retirement Core", "Tech Growth", "Dividend Income", "Earnings Plays" — make it clear what each portfolio is for.
  • Each portfolio is isolated. A stock can't be in two portfolios simultaneously. This keeps your thinking clean.

Write for Yourself, Not the AI

  • Thesis points are your notes. Make them clear to future-you, not optimized for the algorithm.
  • Use domain-specific language if that's how you think. "Cash burn rate below $X" is fine if you're a venture investor.
  • Avoid vague statements. "Strong fundamentals" teaches you nothing; "FCF conversion ratio >30%" is actionable.

Use Your Profile to Recognize Bias

  • Your Bias Fingerprint is a mirror. If you scored high on Anchoring, be especially careful not to fixate on an old price target.
  • Pre-commit to the scenarios. Review your behavioral scenarios monthly. When you encounter one in real life (e.g., a 20% drop), you've already committed to how you'll respond rationally.
  • Use your archetype as a reality check. If you're a "Cautious Income Builder" but you're building a portfolio of small-cap growth stocks, notice the mismatch. There's nothing wrong with it, but be intentional.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Over-personalizing. Don't add a thesis point just because you like the CEO. Build your thesis on repeatable, verifiable facts.
  • Ignoring the bear case. If you can't articulate strong risks for a stock you own, your thesis is incomplete. Add risk points to the "Risks & Bear Case" category.
  • Never re-evaluating. A thesis score from 6 months ago is stale. Re-evaluate regularly so your scores stay current.
  • Freezing on emotion. Freeze core convictions when you're calm and analytical, not after a down day. Emotional freezing often leads to regret.
  • Ignoring importance levels. Mark truly critical points with the Critical flag. If everything is important, nothing is.

Backtesting Panel — Conviction vs Returns

The Conviction vs Returns collapsible panel on the stock detail page answers: "Have my high-conviction scores actually predicted good returns?"

How It Works

  • ThesisArc looks at all your past evaluations for the stock and the price at each evaluation date.
  • It then checks what the price was 30, 90, and 180 days after each evaluation.
  • It calculates a Pearson correlation between your conviction score at evaluation time and the forward return.

Reading the Results

CorrelationInterpretation
+0.7 to +1.0Your conviction scores are strong leading indicators for this stock
+0.3 to +0.7Moderate positive relationship — your thesis has some predictive value
−0.3 to +0.3No clear relationship — keep evaluating to build a longer track record
Below −0.3Negative correlation — your scores may be contrarian indicators (or too few data points)
Caveat: Backtesting requires at least 3–4 evaluations before the correlation is meaningful. Don't read too much into results from a single evaluation.

Categories

Use case: Spot concentration risk — if 80% of your "Valuation" points read "undervalued", you may be taking more P/E risk than you realize.

Streak Badge & Thesis Quiz

Streak Tracker

The streak badge (flame icon in the nav bar) tracks how many consecutive days you've run at least one evaluation. Streaks encourage the habit of regular portfolio check-ins.

  • Badge glows orange at 7+ days and turns gold at 30+ days.
  • Missing a day resets your streak — evaluate at least one stock to keep it alive.
  • Long streaks indicate high-quality, up-to-date thesis scores across your portfolio.

Thesis Quiz

Click the Brain button on the dashboard to open the Thesis Quiz. A stock from your portfolio appears with 4 possible thesis statements — choose the one that actually belongs to that stock.

  • Uses your actual thesis points as both correct and distractor options.
  • Tests recall — can you remember why you own what you own?
  • Score is shown after each question. Track your recall accuracy over time.
Why this matters: Studies show that investors who can articulate their thesis from memory are significantly less likely to panic-sell. The quiz builds that muscle.

Decision Journal

Closing a thesis is just as important as opening one. When a thesis has run its course — played out, broke, or is no longer relevant — close it and write down what you learned. Every closed thesis appears in the cross-portfolio Journal (left nav).

Closing a thesis

From the Thesis Manager, hover any active thesis and click the archive icon. You'll be asked to pick one of four outcomes and write at least 10 characters of lessons:

OutcomeUse when
Played outThe thesis point was confirmed by what actually happened.
PartialSome of the thesis was right, some wasn't.
BrokeThe evidence said the opposite of what you believed.
InvalidatedThe world changed; this thesis no longer applies.

Why this matters

Closed theses stop counting toward your health score, so the score reflects what you currently believe — not the full archaeological record. But the journal keeps the record, with the outcome, the date, and your write-up. Over time the distribution of outcomes is a calibration signal: if almost everything "played out," you're not taking enough risk; if most theses broke, your pattern recognition on that category needs work.

Reopening

If you close a thesis prematurely, you can reopen it from the Thesis Manager ("Show closed" toggle) — the outcome and lessons are cleared and it re-enters scoring.

The point isn't to be right. It's to get better at knowing when you're wrong. The lessons field rewards honesty, not vindication.

Privacy: Share links expose your thesis publicly. Only share links for stocks you're comfortable discussing publicly. Revoke the link at any time from the same menu.
